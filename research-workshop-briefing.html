Building the UK Neuromorphic Computing Roadmap

Outcomes from the NeuroSYNC Research Workshop — 29 April 2026, Aston University, Birmingham

On 29 April 2026, NeuroSYNC brought together researchers, engineers and industry partners from across the UK neuromorphic community to stress-test draft content for the UK Neuromorphic Computing Research Roadmap — a living, ten-year roadmap of the research foundations for brain-inspired computing. This briefing summarises what the community told us, and how it is shaping the roadmap ahead of its first open-access release, planned for late 2026.

Why neuromorphic computing matters

Neuromorphic, or brain-inspired, computing is an emerging technology that takes inspiration from how biological nervous systems process information. By bringing memory and processing together — as neurons and synapses do — and by computing only when events occur, neuromorphic systems can run AI and complex data-processing tasks with very low latency while using a fraction of the energy required by conventional computers.

This matters because demand for AI is pushing today's computing technology, and the energy systems behind it, to their limits. The global neuromorphic computing market is forecast to grow from $5.3 billion in 2023 to $20 billion by 2030[1], driven by demand for low-power, real-time AI in sectors such as next-generation semiconductors, telecommunications, healthcare, robotics and autonomous systems.

"Future computing paradigms [such as] neuromorphic computing, inspired by the brain's really remarkable energy efficiency, could fundamentally transform how AI operate"

Lord Vallance, UK Science Minister[2]

A national research roadmap, built with the community

NeuroSYNC, the UK Multidisciplinary Centre for Neuromorphic Systems and Computing, is led by Aston University and backed by a £5.6 million investment from UK Research & Innovation (UKRI). The Centre is developing the UK Neuromorphic Computing Roadmap: a near-term, living roadmap setting out the core research foundations for the field over the next ten years. It will be published open access, updated annually to reflect the rapid pace of the field, and selected chapters will be developed for peer-reviewed publication.

The research roadmap is structured around six interconnected chapters, and sits alongside the longer-term, policy-facing roadmap 'Neuromorphic Technologies: A Roadmap to 2050', led by the NeuroWare Innovation & Knowledge Centre. Together, the two roadmaps form a common strategic framework for UK leadership in brain-inspired computing.

The workshop: inviting challenge, not validation

The Research Workshop, held at Aston University in Birmingham on 29 April 2026, was deliberately designed as a working day rather than a showcase. Draft chapter summaries and discussion questions were circulated in advance, and each of the six chapter sessions combined a short presentation with structured table discussion and report-back, closing with a cross-chapter integration session on system-level dependencies and gaps. The room brought together physicists, engineers, neuroscientists, materials scientists, computer scientists and industry participants — exactly the breadth a multidisciplinary roadmap needs.

"We are not presenting finished chapters. We are stress-testing draft content against expert scrutiny — and we are looking for genuine feedback and challenge, not validation."

The workshop was not intended to produce consensus on every question, and in several areas significant differences in emphasis and strategic direction remain. Its purpose was to identify where the community is converging, where uncertainty remains, and where further evidence and discussion are needed.

What happens next

Chapter teams are revising their drafts to reflect the workshop discussion. Where the day surfaced the need for deeper, specialist conversation, dedicated follow-on workshops will be convened — beginning with materials and devices, run jointly with NeuroWare and NeuMat, and continuing with chapter-specific sessions for algorithms, electronics, photonics and neuroscience. The roadmap is a living document: the first published version will not be the final word, but the version mature enough to invite the next round of challenge.

Get involved

The roadmap is being built for the UK community as a whole, not for any one institution — and it is strengthened by constructive disagreement. We welcome written feedback on any chapter direction, suggestions of missing topics and under-represented UK capabilities, and expressions of interest in chapter-specific follow-on workshops. Industry and end-user partners are particularly invited to propose candidate 'killer applications' and to share realistic deployment constraints — precision, latency, power envelope and integration requirements — which shape the chapters directly.
