Community Engagement

What the community told us

Ahead of drafting, NeuroSYNC ran a stakeholder survey to capture priorities and perspectives from across the UK neuromorphic community before the six research chapters and the industrial applications chapter were written. 56 people responded — researchers and academics, engineers, industry R&D leads, policy and regulatory contacts, and end-users — and their answers shaped both the chapter drafts and the workshop agendas that followed.

Who responded

56 responses, overwhelmingly from researchers and academics, and overwhelmingly UK-based — with a global vantage point on the field's own early stage of maturity.

Gaps and disagreements

Asked directly where the UK stands globally in neuromorphic computing, respondents did not agree — and the spread is itself a finding. The same question produced answers from "leading" to "pretty low down" to "behind the US, China and Germany":

"{{ q }}"

That divergence recurs elsewhere: on required funding scale, estimates ranged from "a couple of million pounds annually" for a single research programme to calls for £500M–£1B over 5–10 years at a national level. There's no shared read on where the UK sits, or what it would take to change that — which is itself a gap the roadmap needs to name rather than paper over.

What this shaped

Survey input fed directly into the roadmap's six research chapters — brain-inspired computing and spiking/plasticity-based approaches came through as the clear scope priority, ahead of analogue/in-memory computing and translation to industry — and into the discussion questions circulated before the Research and Industry workshops. The gaps identified here, particularly around the UK's global position and the scale of funding needed, are being carried forward into the cross-chapter integration work and the case the roadmap makes to funders.
